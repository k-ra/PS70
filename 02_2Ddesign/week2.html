week 2: 2D Design

HeartBox


Tuesday, Jan 30th Autodesk finds me troublesome

My journey begins from behind the start line, where I had to wrangle with Autodesk for approximately a week. I briefly learned some Rhino, which means I learned how to make sketches and transform them! I tried to import a vector drawing as a DXF file, to get around the temporary software lock-up. I learn that the laser cutting settings here are slightly different than the ones in the SEC, where I previously printed little trinkets directly from vector files in pngs (Figure 1: my tiny tigers). After drawing a little hungry bunny as a keychain and importing it directly into Rhino, I realize that rasters don't print directly (at least I didn't know a way), and everything has to be a vector in order to be marked 'CUT', 'SCORE', or 'ENGRAVE'.



Thursday, Feb 1st I overcome it with love

I sketched this heartbox! It has to be done in two pieces, since the flatlay of the box construction overlaps in a couple of places. The squiggly lines indicate places where the fingerlocking would take place. The lines within the heart indicate my original design for compartments, which I ran out of time to actually execute but will be circling back to implement. I really liked this process of designing the flatlay of a non-rectangular prism! Another option was to make it an actual curved symbol of a heart and use the scoring technique to bend cardboard. However, I decided to try fingerlocking to join the rest of the class!

You can see my original design for the top would have two wings sticking out as handles, and the lid inset within the box. There would be notches cut into the sides of the heart for the handles to rest. However, I eventually leaned towards the flip-up design!

Tuesday, Feb 6th: I design the thing

My original sketch had wings as handles, with the wings resting on notches on the sides. Unfortunately, I found myself a little culturally distanced from the "heart with wings" notion, and was unsure if I was really that type of person. Hence I decided for a simple peep-hole with a modern curved heart. It serves the purpose, as it indicates that the box is a heart, not a right angle or (worse) an L!

Thursday, Feb 8th: Translating into reality!

The fingerlocking design I landed on was more minimalistic, with three joints per edge! I measured the thickness of the box and made the height of everything slightly above that (to account for error and for fire burning the material away)./
At the laser printer, I spent a good few hours orienting the design, as it kept printing two times smaller! I discovered it was because someone prior had shifted the printer settings. Shout out to Nathan for discovering this!

I also learned that the laser printer software, UVX, treats whatever perspective is frozen on the screen at the time of "Command P" as the actual flatlay design. Due to this, my first print was two times smaller AND slightly less than orthogonal. All the angles were infinitessimally off.
Which means the entire plan printed as if I was a slightly off-center bird, looking down. It sported these skewed angles, 96 instead of 90 degrees, imperceptively off-putting.After diagnosing this, I was able to execute the same fixes from then onwards!
Assembly was rather painless, though I should have creased it before putting it together! Because the material longed to flatten, I put discreet little dots of hot glue at the corners to keep it sturdy. Then I was done! Phew!

Fusing Fusion with Reality

I decided to make this dining hall cup, a shape that feels like it belongs in a nursery, a home, and a nursing home:

I've always found it off putting, so I appreciated getting the chance to know it better. Now I think it's comfortingly strange. It's not wobbly enough to be entirely abstracted into a shape, and not finger-memory enough to be ergonomic. It's not consistent with its diameters, as it starts thin at the bottom and slowly ends up expanding, only to contract imperceptibly at the top. The squiggle is dramatic but not enough to be a doll-house toy. It's everything in between. Thank you!
